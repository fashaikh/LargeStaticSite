Blacklock (1814 – 24 February 1831) was a British Thoroughbred racehorse. He raced from 1816 to 1819 and won seventeen of his twenty-three races, including three Great Subscription Purses and the York Gold Cup. After retiring from racing he became a successful stallion and was British Champion sire in 1829, the year his son Voltaire won the Doncaster Cup. During his racing career he was trained by Tommy Sykes. He was owned by Thomas Kirby as a two-year-old and by Richard Watt for the rest of his career.

[edit] Background

Blacklock was a bay colt bred by Francis Moss and foaled in 1814.[1] He was sired by Whitelock, who won a few races in the north of England. Whitelock was a son of St. Leger and dual Doncaster Cup winner Hambletonian, who was only defeated once in his career.[2] His dam was a daughter of Coriander. Blacklock was the seventh of her nine foals, the youngest of which being 1822 St. Leger winner Theodore.[3] Francis Moss had bought Blacklock's dam for £3 in 1803.[2]

Blacklock was not thought to be a good looking horse. He was described as having "a head like a half-moon" and being calf-kneed.[2] Thomas Kirby purchased him from Moss for £40.[2]

[edit] Racing career

[edit] 1816: Two-year-old season

Blacklock made his racecourse debut 23 August at York in a Sweepstakes of 20 guineas for two-year-olds. After starting at the price of about 3/1 he won the race, with the judge being unable to place any of his five rivals.[4] On 11 September 1816 at Pontefract, he faced three opponents for a sweepstakes of 20 guineas each over one mile. He started as the 13/8 favourite and won the race. Shylock finished in second place, with Angelica in third.[5] Blacklock was then purchased by Richard Watt.[6] Racing in Watt's colours (Harlequin)[7] and ridden by jockey J. Jackson, Blacklock made his final start as a two-year-old at Doncaster on the 24th September when he competed in another sweepstakes of 20 guineas each. He started as the 4/7 favourite, he won the race from the Young Woodpecker colt, who was followed by Eglinton.[8] Blacklock won the race easily.[6]

[edit] 1817: Three-year-old season

His first race as a three-year-old was the St. Leger Stakes at Doncaster on Monday 22 September. Blacklock didn't arrive at Doncaster until the Saturday, and after reports that he was amiss he had drifted out to as big as 10/1 in the betting. After arriving on Saturday he had a gallop in the afternoon and he soon shortened back up it the betting. After another of the pre-race favourites, Stainborough, was withdrawn due to illness Blacklock was sent off as the short-priced favourite at about evens. In the final furlong of the race he had a slight lead and he battled with Restless and 20/1 outsider Ebor as they approached the finish. Ebor came out on top, beating Blacklock by a neck.[9] Restless finished in third place and was the only other runner that could be placed by the judge.[10] Blacklock had a crack in one of his hind heels, which was thought to have affected him in the race.[9]

Two days later he faced St. Helena over the same course and distance in the Gascoigne Stakes,[11] which he won easily.[12] The day after the Gascoigne Stakes he lost to the 1816 St. Leger winner, The Duchess, in the Doncaster Club Stakes over two miles.[13] On 8 October at Richmond he won a sweepstakes of 20 guineas each, beating four rivals, with Boroughman finishing second.[14] Later in the day he won the Dundas Stakes, beating Rasping, D.I.O and Shepard into second, third and fourth respectively.[15]

Engraving of Blacklock

[edit] 1818: Four-year-old season

He started the 1818 season much earlier than he had done the previous two seasons, with his first race coming on 18 May at the York spring meeting in a sweepstakes of 20 guineas each over two mile. He started as the 1/2 favourite, but could only finish third behind St. Helena.[16] Two days later he started as the 4/6 favourite for the Constitution Stakes over a mile and a quarter. He biggest rival was expected to be the Duke of Leeds's Rasping, who was priced at 2/1. Blacklock won the race from Rasping, with Hornby in third and Whiff last of the four runners.[17]

Blacklock didn't race again until August at York, where he ran in the four-mile Great Subscription Purse for four-year-olds. He started as the evens favourite and faced three rivals; Agatha, St. Helena and a filly by Orville.[18] Blacklock won the race by over 100 yards without even being asked for an effort, causing some people to proclaim "nothing has been seen at all equal to Mr. Watt's Blacklock since the days of Eclipse."[19] This referring to the ease with which Eclipse won his races. Agatha finished the race in second place and St. Helena in third.[18] The race was won in a time of 7 minutes 23 seconds.[19] The next day he beat Silenus to win the four-mile Great Subscription Purse for four and five-year-olds. Later in the day he started as the 1/2 favourite in a two-mile sweepstakes of 25 guineas each, where he faced four opponents. Despite it being his third race in two days he won. Rasping finished second, St. Helena third, Mandeville fourth and Skipjack fifth.[20]

He then went to Doncaster where, on 23 September, he started 1/2 favourite and beat The Duchess to win the Doncaster Stakes over four miles.[21] The same day he also walked over for a sweepstakes of 50 guineas each over the St. Leger course. The next day he beat Rasping to win a sweepstakes of 25 guineas each over four miles.[22] Later in the day, Blacklock (who started as the 1/8 favourite) beat The Duchess to win the Doncaster Club Stakes. This was his fourth race in the space of two days.[23] At Richmond in October he won his second Dundas Stakes, this time beating King Corney. Later in the day he finished last of four runners behind winner Doctor Syntax in the Richmond Cup over four miles.[24] Doctor Syntax had started in the lead, but was overtaken by Blacklock after only 200 yards. Blacklock held the lead until about half a mile from the finish, when Doctor Syntax joined him again. Blacklock then swerved out of line, allowing Doctor Syntax to win easily.[25] By the time The Richmond Cup was run Blacklock was apparently unwell and was coughing repeatedly during the race.[26]

[edit] 1819: Five-year-old season

On 17 May 1819 at York, Blacklock finished second of seven in a two-mile sweepstakes of 20 guineas each. The race was won by The Marshall.[27] On 18 May he started as the evens favourite for the two-mile Gold Cup. He won the race, with Paulowitz finishing second, Torch-bearer third and Otho fourth.[28] At the York August meeting Blacklock faced three rivals in the four-mile Great Subscription Purse for five-year-olds and older. The Duchess started as the 5/2 favourite, with Blacklock and St. Helena both at 3/1 and Magistrate at 4/1. Blacklock won the race from Magistrate, with The Duchess finishing in third place.[29] Blacklock's final race came two days later, when he finished second to St. Helena in a sweepstakes of 25 guineas each over two miles.[30]

[edit] Stud career

Blacklock's son Velocipede
Blacklock's son Voltaire

Blacklock was retired to stud, where he became a successful stallion. He first stood at Bishop Burton in Yorkshire, with his fee initially set at 15 guineas and half a guinea for the groom (Thomas Barrow).[33] He was then leased to Thomas Kirkby in York for four seasons where he stood for a much lower fee.[2] In 1827 he was standing at Bildeston in Suffolk for a fee of twelve guineas.[34] His fee reached up to 25 guineas.[2]

Blacklock was Champion sire of Great Britain in 1829.[35] His progeny included:

  • Brutandorf (1821) – won the Tradesmen's Cup and Stand Cup at Chester in 1826.[36] As a sire he produced the Grand National winner Gaylad and Hetman Platoff, who won several cups. He was also grandsire of Derby winner Cossack.[37]
  • Brownlock (1822) – won 25 races.[38]
  • Belzoni (1823) – won the York St. Leger[36] and later became a successful sire of hunters. He produced Vanguard, who won the Grand National in 1843.[39]
  • Laurel (1824) – won the Doncaster Cup in 1828. Through one of his unnamed daughters he was the damsire of Oaks winners Rhedycina and Governess.[37]
  • Robin Hood (1825) – won ten races[38] including two Newcastle Gold Cups.[37]
  • Belinda (1825) – finished second in the St. Leger at Doncaster. As a broodmare she foaled Gimcrack Stakes winner Tuscan, as well as Lollypop, who became the dam of Doncaster Cup winner Sweetmeat.[2]
  • Miss Pratt (1825) – foaled Echidna, who was the dam of The Baron. The Baron won the St. Leger and sired the influential stallion Stockwell.[2]
  • Velocipede (1825) – won the York St. Leger, York Gold Cup and Liverpool Cup. He sired Derby winner Amato, Epsom Oaks and St Leger winner Queen of Trumps and 2000 Guineas winner Meteor.[40]
  • Tranby (1826) – won the Oatlands Stakes in 1832 and ran four four-mile legs in George Osbaldeston's successful attempt to ride 200 miles in 10 hours.[41]
  • Voltaire (1826) – won the Doncaster Gold Cup and finished second in the St. Leger in 1829.[42] He sired Derby and St. Leger winner Voltigeur. Voltigeur's son Vedette was the grandsire of the undefeated St. Simon, who became Champion sire nine times. It is mainly through St. Simon that Blacklock's sire line survives today.
  • Moss Rose (1827) – won the Dee Stakes at Chester.
  • Belshazzar (1830) – finished third in an Ascot Gold Cup. He sired 1000 Guineas winner Cara and was later sent to America.[39]
  • Blacklock mare – foaled Progress, who was the dam of Derby winner Attila.[2]

Blacklock died on 24 February 1831 after rupturing a blood vessel when covering a mare.[38] In total Blacklock sired the winners of 442 races and over £50,000.[2]
